Sorta — sorting
out food waste

Rethinking food waste behaviour through simple, intentional interaction design.

01 · The Context

Households waste a third of the food they buy.

Food waste is a massive environmental and economic problem — but most of it happens quietly at home. People know they're wasting food, but between busy schedules, forgetfulness, and cluttered fridges, nothing changes.


My goal wasn't to guilt-trip or lecture. It was to understand why the behaviour persists, and design something that gently shifts it.

"I always mean to use things up before they go bad. And then I open the fridge on Friday and it's too late." — Research participant, household of 2
02 · Research

Finding the real problem

I ran 6 semi-structured interviews with people aged 20–45, alongside a 2-week diary study where participants photographed their bins before taking out the rubbish. This revealed patterns that a survey alone would've missed.

5/6 Participants felt guilt — but no agency

They knew waste was happening but didn't feel like they had control over it. The problem felt inevitable.

83% Waste happened to forgotten items at the back of the fridge

"Out of sight, out of mind" was the dominant pattern. No existing habit triggered a fridge inventory.

0/6 No one planned meals around what they already had

Shopping and cooking were disconnected. People bought new ingredients without checking what needed using up.

03 · Problem Framing

From insights to direction

The research made clear: this isn't a knowledge gap. People know food waste is bad. It's a salience problem — food expires out of awareness, not out of intent. The intervention needed to happen at the right moment, with low friction.

How Might We

How might we make the most-at-risk food visible at the moment a person is deciding what to eat — so using it up feels like the easiest choice, not a sacrifice?

04 · Design Process

How I got from question to concept

01
Behavioural mapping

I mapped the full food cycle — from shopping through storage to cooking — to identify every moment where awareness could be introduced without feeling intrusive. A journey map helped me spot the two biggest intervention windows: after grocery shopping and at the "what's for dinner?" moment.

02
Competitive analysis + precedent review

I reviewed 8 existing apps (Too Good To Go, NoWaste, BeforeDinner) and identified where they fell short: too much manual entry friction, no contextual nudges, no connection to cooking. I also reviewed research on commitment devices and fresh-start effects to ground my approach in behavioural science.

03
Sketching + low-fi wireframes

I prioritised one core flow: adding food → getting a timely nudge → acting on a recipe suggestion. I sketched 12 variations of the home dashboard before landing on a "traffic light" expiry model that tested best in a quick paper prototype session.

04
Usability testing (2 rounds)

I tested with 5 participants per round using Maze. Round 1 revealed confusion around the "sort by urgency" interaction — users missed it entirely. Round 2 tested a redesigned dashboard where urgent items surfaced automatically. Task completion improved from 60% to 92%.

05 · The Design

Simple surfaces, smart defaults

Three screens do the heavy lifting. The design intentionally reduces choice — rather than giving users a full fridge inventory to manage, Sorta surfaces only what matters right now.

06 · Key Design Decisions

Why these choices matter

No gamification, no streaks

Research showed users resented feeling judged. I removed any scoring or "waste saved" metrics from the primary UI — they're in a secondary stats view only. The main interface stays neutral and practical.

Barcode scan over manual entry

Manual entry was the #1 drop-off point in existing apps. Sorta's primary add method is barcode scan + camera date recognition, reducing add time from ~40 seconds to under 8 in testing.

Push at the right moment, not constantly

Notifications are sent once, at 5pm — the "what's for dinner" moment. Participants in testing said this felt helpful rather than naggy, compared to apps that notify at item expiry only.

Traffic-light hierarchy for urgency

Colour alone wasn't enough (accessibility). I paired red/amber/green with explicit time labels ("expires today", "2 days left") so the urgency is legible without needing to interpret the colour.

07 · Outcomes

What the testing showed

92%

Task completion on the core "add food → get nudge → find recipe" flow in round 2 usability testing. Up from 60% after the dashboard redesign.

8s

Average time to add a food item via barcode scan — compared to 40s with manual entry alternatives.

5/5

Usability test participants said they'd use the notification timing (5pm) rather than turn it off. Zero requested fewer notifications.

08 · Reflection

What I'd do differently

This project pushed my thinking on how to design for behaviour change without being prescriptive. A few honest reflections:

I'd recruit more diverse participants earlier. My research skewed toward 20-somethings living alone or in pairs. Households with kids have a totally different food management dynamic — I'd want to explore that before any real development phase.

The diary study was the most valuable method. It felt like extra work to set up, but it revealed behaviour I never would have caught in interviews — people dramatically underreported how much they wasted when asked directly.

I under-designed the add-food experience. I focused so much on the dashboard that the entry flow felt rushed in hindsight. The next iteration would prototype multiple scanning interaction patterns with real test users.

I'd measure intention vs. actual behaviour over time. All my data is from sessions, not longitudinal. The real test of a behaviour-change product is whether the habit sticks — something I couldn't fully evaluate in a student project timeframe.
